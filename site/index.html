What do AI models think these companies are worth?

We asked 26 AI models — with knowledge cutoffs from September 2021 to February 2026, and no internet access — what OpenAI, Anthropic, NVIDIA, Alphabet and Meta would be worth on each January 1 from 2024 to 2030. The story in one line: the models forecast like a finance textbook — sensible growth rates, the right distribution shapes — and reality beat their expectations almost every time.

github.com/TrelisResearch/llm-valuation-forecasts · by @ronankmcgovern

1 · What the models predicted — and what happened

Each blue line is a “vintage” of models grouped by the year their knowledge ends (darker = knows more recent history). Red is reality. Every vintage revised upward as its knowledge advanced — and reality still escaped above the lot. Log scale, USD billions; dates a model could already know are excluded.

2 · The models weren’t guessing — they priced like a finance textbook

Convert each vintage’s forecast curve into an annual growth rate and a clean pattern appears: ~8–10%/yr for the public companies (the “required rate of return” — in an efficient market, faster predictable growth would already be in the price) and venture-style rates for the startups. Reality (red) grew several times faster than any of it.

3 · They even believe the “right” shapes: startups are power-law, stocks are lognormal

We asked each model: what’s the chance the company is worth 1.5×, 2×, 5×… 100× today’s value in 2030? Plotted on log-log axes, a straight line means a power law. For Anthropic the models draw near-straight lines with tail exponent α ≈ 1.4–2.0 — the same range measured for real venture returns. For NVIDIA the curves bend downward: thinner, lognormal-like tails, the standard model for public equities. Hover any point for exact values.

4 · So why do their answers look so rosy? Ask for one number and the risk disappears

The same model, the same question, asked two ways. Asked for a single “best estimate” of a 2030 valuation (green dot), a model tells a smooth success story. Asked for its full range of outcomes — 10th percentile, median, 90th percentile (blue band and dot) — the same model admits enormous uncertainty, including a large chance the company is worth less than today (dashed line). If you use an LLM’s single-number answer, you inherit a forecast that quietly assumes things go well. (The green dots also sit low because those questions gave no current valuation, so models anchored on older knowledge.)

Chance the company is worth less in 2030 than today — according to the models

For a fairly-priced, high-volatility asset this should sit around 35–45% (shaded band) — and the models mostly agree. None of this risk is visible in any single-number answer they give.

5 · The catch: reality beat even their generous ranges

For dates that have already happened (Jan 2024 → Jul 2026), we asked models for their 10th/50th/90th percentile ranges and checked where the real valuation landed. A well-calibrated forecaster is beaten above its 90th percentile ~10% of the time (top reference row). It happened 69% of the time — 85% for the startups. Right theory, wrong magnitude.
